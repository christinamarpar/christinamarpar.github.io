User Interview Advice


  • Eric Migicovsky (YC) - How to Talk to Users
    • Notes:

    • Important for founders to maintain a direct connection to users
      • No exception for technical founders
      • Everyone must learn to communicate well and prioritize interaction with users

    • Common Mistakes with user interviews:
      • Pointers synthesized from The Mom Test by Rob Fitzpatrick (YC alum)
      • Upshot: people who love and want to support us (i.e. parents/friends/family) are generally poor resources for honest/critical feedback
      • Key Ideas:
        1. Talk about their life, not your idea

          • Group 1 (The ): User has already taken FL classes, but is not yet fluent
            • When learning a FL in the past: What did you enjoy?
            • What did you not enjoy?
            • Have you ever picked up studying again for a period of time (including currently)?
            • If not currently studying:
            • How did you study?
            • What did you like about what you tried?
            • What did you not like?
            • What kept you motivated to learn for that period of time?
            • What made you stop or hit pause?
            • Did you spend any money on study tools or classes?
            • How much did you spend, and on what tools or classes?
            • How many times have you started studying again?
            • Have you spent money each of those times? How have your spending habits changed?
            • Have any of these attempts been more successful than the others? If so, why?
            • What, if anything, motivates you to try again?
            • Have you ever tried reading books in your FL? If yes, what was that experience like?
            • Do you currently pay money for any recurring language-learning subscriptions? If so, how much?

          • Group 2 (The Chante): User is fluent in a FL, but would like to improve
            • How did you reach your current level in your FL?
            • What kept you motivated to study?
            • What were your most effective study strategies?
            • What did you like about those strategies?
            • What did you not like?
            • How much money (if any) did you spend on books/apps/classes?
            • How do you continue to study/practice your FL, if at all?
            • Have you ever tried reading books in your FL? If yes, what was that experience like?
            • Do you currently pay money for any recurring language-learning subscriptions? If so, how much?

          • Group 3 (The ): User is a FL teacher or professor (they help others to reach fluency)
            • What level of FL do you teach?
            • What expectations do you have for your students by the end of the year/semester?
            • How closely does your class conform to teaching guidelines and state or federal curriculum?
            • Do you find the guidelines and curriculum to be helpful/effective?
            • What do you personally believe to be the best way to learn a foreign language?
            • Would you structure your class differently if you had the leeway? How so?
            • What gets your students most excited/motivated to learn a FL?
            • What do you think causes some students to learn faster than others?
            • How much of a budget is there for spending on FL-learning activities or tools?
            • How is that money currently used?
            • Is there any restriction on how that money is spent?
            • Have you ever assigned books in your FL class? If yes, what was that experience like?
            • What aspects of learning and teaching a FL bring you joy?

        2. Talk specifics, not hypotheticals (e.g. Would you like this product feature?)
        3. Listen, don't talk.

    • How to identify the best first customer (i.e. the , the Chante, or the ?):
      • Find numerical answers to:
        • How much does this problem cost?
        • How frequent is the problem?
        • How large is their budget?

    • When using surveys for large numbers of people, it helps to collect phone numbers so you can follow up if a survey result confuses or surprises you.

    • Discard bad data:
      1. Compliments
      2. Fluff (hypotheticals i.e. "I would...")
